TOPIC

The importance of teaching the Constitution at all educational levels

Introduction

Just look at a country where most people need to learn what their Constitution entails. How would these people speak out against injustice? How would these people hold their elected officials accountable, and how would they even participate in the democratic process? This lack of knowledge about the Constitution weakens the core of the nation.

The Constitution defines how our government works, protects our freedoms, and sets the rules for a fair and just nation. But how can we expect citizens to uphold these principles if they don't understand them? This why public awareness of the constitution is very important factor to consider to for better democracy country.

In this podcast, I asset that Incorporating Constitutional Studies across the curriculum, from upper elementary through high school grades in Ghana, would foster public awareness and understanding of civil rights and liberties in the next generation.

Importance of constitutional studies in all educational levels
With Petra Asima, Ashesi University

Why teaching the Constitution is important for nation building

Sam Carter. Ghana is a democratic country, and public awareness of the Constitution would have a massive impact on democratic activities. Starting at a young age, students should learn about their rights in detail through interesting stories and simple explanations of the Constitution through case reviews and others. As they get older, the lessons should go into more detail about how the different parts of the government work together and check each other to prevent anyone from having too much power. Making sure all students, no matter their family's income or background, learn about the Constitution, every citizen in the next generation would have the chance to understand and use the rights that the Constitution protects, no matter who they are or where they come from.

Why would studying the Constitution from primary school be an effective way to inform the next generation of Ghanaians about the laws? Elementary, middle, and high schools are the most impactful stages in education that provide the foundational knowledge for everyday life. When the Constitution and laws are taught at these levels, we can grow young minds with a solid foundation in constitutional law. These lessons would form the base of their thinking, moral habits, and social habits in their community. When students have a strong understanding of the Constitution at their early stages, they are better prepared to navigate the complexities of the law, which empowers them to think critically about important issues and actively participate as engaged citizens.

Richard C. Maxwell, a Chadwick Professor Emeritus of Law at Duke University, in his journal article "Law Studies in the Schools," assets that learning about the law can teach students important skills like critical thinking, evaluating evidence, and considering different arguments, looking at past cases, and coming to well-reasoned conclusions. These are valuable skills that aren't limited to just studying the law. However, learning about the law has the added benefit of doing all of this within the context of citizenship and what it means to be a member of society. It connects these important thinking skills directly to the rights, responsibilities, and meaning of being a citizen. No single school subject has a monopoly on teaching these kinds of analytical and reasoning skills. However, studying law-related topics gives students the opportunity to develop them while also grappling with the real-world implications and significance of the law and our legal system.

Again, regarding the importance of studying the Constitution in basic schools to create awareness of the complexity of the legal system, a study published in the International Journal for the Semiotics of Law in 2019 highlights the role of education in fostering legal awareness and understanding of the legal system. The research suggests that early education in law and civic responsibilities can significantly enhance legal awareness among students. Consequently, this implies that when students are well informed about the Constitution in their early years, it helps them make well-informed decisions in their lives. Well-informed citizens about the government and the Constitution can be more active and effective citizens; they can keep an eye on our leaders and make sure they are doing their jobs properly (Shanker Institute, n.d). Informed citizens can also have thoughtful discussions about important issues and stand up for their rights. This helps make our democracy stronger because it means the voices and concerns of everyday people are heard and considered.

Concerns from Ghanaians

People may ask, but wouldn't adding another subject overload the curriculum? I understand the concern about adding another subject to the curriculum. But we can creatively teach about the Constitution without making it a separate subject or a course. We can include constitutional ideas and principles in students' courses, like history, government, and English. This helps students see how the Constitution relates to their everyday lives.

One may argue that Ghana already has Social Studies and RME (Religious and Moral Education) that touch on these topics. I may say while Social Studies and RME talk a bit about the Constitution, we need a dedicated Studies program that can offer a more in-depth and systematic approach. Imagine a Social Studies class that brushes over the Constitution versus a dedicated class that talks in detail about the philosophy behind each right, articles, and sections, explores landmark cases, and debates on relevant court cases.

Conclution

By recognizing the importance of constitutional education at all levels, we can empower citizens, foster active citizenship, and strengthen democratic values. The government should take a proactive step to ensure that every citizen can learn and understand the Constitution. With this, we can pave the way for a more informed, engaged, and law-abiding society where individuals actively contribute to the betterment of the nation.
